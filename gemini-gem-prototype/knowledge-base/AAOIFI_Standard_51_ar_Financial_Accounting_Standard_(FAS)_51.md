 Preface  
 The foundation of Islamic finance is based on risk sharing. Amongst various products of 
Islamic finance, those based on Shari’ah -compliant participatory or profit and loss sharing 
(or taking) structures (including, Mudaraba and Musharaka ) are considered very significant, 
and by some as most preferred . AAOIFI had always given due significance to these products 
since its inception and the AAOIFI Financial Accounting Standard  (FAS) 3 “Mudaraba 
Financing” and FAS 4 “Musharaka Financing”  addressing the accountin g and financial 
reporting for such products, were amongst the initially issued standards.   
 The Islamic finance industry , since then,  has grown  significantly  in terms of size , as well as 
the product structures , the avenues of usage  and forms of  such products.  Accordingly , there 
was a need to review existing standards , and to align the same with  the new “AAOIFI 
Conceptual Framework  for Financial Reporting ” and FAS 1  “General Presentation and 
Disclosures in the Financial Statements” , as well as, with the global best practices .  
 This project forms  part of the comprehensive project  undertaken by the AAOIFI Accounting 
Board ( AAB / the board) for review and revision of FASs  issued earlier . 
 This standard supersede s the existing FAS  3 “Mudaraba Financing ” and FAS 4 “Musharaka 
Financing ”.

 Introduction  
Overview  
IN1 This standard  prescribes accounting and financial reporting principles for recognition , 
measurement,  presentation and disclosures to apply in relation to participatory venture s 
(including most of the common structures / products based on Mudaraba and Musharaka) . 
IN2 This standard shall  be appl ied in respect of accounting and financial reporting by the 
investor (s), the working partner(s), as well as, the participatory ventures  with regard to all 
such participatory ventures  that fall within the scope of this standard , including those with 
fixed and variable equity  / quasi -equity shares.   
Rationale  for issuing this standard  
IN3 In line with t he new strategy of the board, AAOIFI initiated the “AAOIFI Financial Accounting 
Standards (FAS s) review and revision project” in 2018 to review and revise all existing FASs. 
As part of  the process and in view of the feedback received from market participants , it was 
considered imperative to perform a comprehensive revision of the existing AAOIFI FASs on 
Mudaraba and Musharaka , in addition to the proposed revision of FAS 27 “Investment 
Accounts” .  
IN4 A revision of these standards was considered pertinent because the same had been issued 
quite a long time back and ever since there have been significant developments in the 
structure and application of the products based on Musharaka and Mudaraba, as well as, in 
the financial reporting standards applied to similar transactions and structures across the 
industry , globally . Furthermore , and as a result of the finalisation and issuance of the 
“AAOIFI Conceptual Framework for Financial Reporting”1 (the conceptual framework)  and 
the FAS 1 “General Presentation and Disclosures in the Financial Statements”2, it was 
inevitable to ensure that all earlier issued FASs are also aligned with the new  
pronouncements.  Particularly, certain accounting treatments prescribed by the earlier FASs 
were not considered aligned with the  conceptual  framework and the generally accepted 
accounting principles, and hence, the ir alignment with the same w as considered necessary.  
Significant changes from previous standard s 
IN5 This standard introduces significant improvements to its predecessor standard s (namely, FAS 
3 “Mudaraba Financing” and FAS 4 “Musharaka Financing”), inter alia, in the following 
aspects:  
a. the existing standards did not cover the cases of participatory ventures in the books 
of account of the  working partner (s) and the  financial reporting for the  participatory  
venture itself ;

Objective of the standard  
1. The objective of this standard is to prescribe the principles of accounting and financial 
reporting  for the participatory ventures .  
Scope  
2. This standard shall apply to participatory ventures  (see paragraph  4 (e)), except for the 
exclusions provided in paragraph 3, in respect of accounting and financial reporting by the 
investor (s), the working partner(s) , as well as, the participatory ventures . 
3. This standard shall not apply to the following :  
a. investment accounts and similar participatory instruments  issued by Islamic banks 
and similar financial institutions which are subject to the requirements of relevant 
AAOIFI FAS s3; 
b. investment agency ( Al-Wakala Bi Al -Istithmar ) which is subject to requirements of 
respective AAOIFI FAS4; 
c. co-owned assets ( based on Shirkat -ul-Milk)5; 
d. participants’ investment funds and other similar participatory instruments issued by 
Takaful institutions to the Takaful participants which are subject to the requirements 
of relevant AAOIFI FASs6; 
e. investment structures in the form of  a legal entity, e.g., those including:  
i. issuance of share capital or other similar instruments by the working 
partner(s)  or by a new (jointly -owned) legal entity ; 
ii. Sukuk or other similar instrument s which are subject to accounting in line 
with the requirements of the relevant AAOIFI FAS s7, except for the 
accounting and financial reporting by the originator in respect of Sukuk 
based on participatory ventures, to the extent of underlying product / asset 
accounting as referred back to this standard ;

Definitions  
4. For the purpose of interpreting and applying this standard, the following short definitions

Accounting by the investor under pass -through accounting approach  
Initial r ecognition  
10. On initial recognition using the pass -through accounting approach, the investor shall 
recognise the assets (as well as , liabilities, if any) underlying the participatory venture in its 
books of account applying the initial recognition principles as applicable in line with relevant 
AAOIFI FAS s13. Proportionate assets (and liabilities) shall be recognised, applying the same 
principles, in cases where the arrangement has more than one investor.  
Subsequent measurement  
11. Subsequent to initial recognition, the underlying assets (and liabilities) shall be measured 
according to the requirements of relevant AAOIFI FAS s14. Proportionate accounting shall 
apply, where applicable. [Explanation: These shall be recognised on a gross basis by the 
investor  (i.e., assets to be included in assets, and liabilities, if any, to be included in 
liabilities].  
12. An investor shall determine, at each period end, impairment in the value of investment 
under pass -through  accounting  approach in line with the requirements of respective AAOIFI 
FAS15. The settlement risk , as well as, existence of onerous commitments (e.g., a guarantee 
or a sale undertaking), shall also be considered for impairment  assessment  purposes . 
Revenues  and expenses  
13. Unless contrary to a specific requirement of this standard, the resulting revenues, expenses, 
gains and losses, arising from the participatory venture , shall be measured and accounted 
for, respectively, according to the nature of the contract(s) in line with the requirements of 
relevant AAOIFI FASs applicable to such transactions16. Normal accounting policies of the 
investor shall apply in respect of all such matters.  
14. Revenues, expenses, gains and losses shall be recognised on a gross basis (i.e., the same 
shall be included in respective line items in the financial statements of the investor).  
Working partner ’s share of profit  
15. The working partner ’s share of profit payable, according to the contractual arrangements, 
shall be recognised as an expense at the end of each financial reporting period , based on the 
best estimate of amounts payable, as at the respective period end, based on contractual and 
constructive obligations.

Accounting by the investor under net investment in participatory venture 
accounting approach  
Initial recognition  
21. While applying the net investment in participatory venture  accounting  approach , an investor 
shall initially recognise the investment made as an investment in a participatory venture at 
cost, at the time when it is made available to the prospective working partner  for 
investment.  
22. In case of investment in -kind, the initial recognition shall be based on the fair value of the in -
kind investment, as at the date of investment.  
23. In case the investor has made investment available to the prospective working partner( s), 
but the contract is yet to be initiated, such amount shall be  initially recognised as an advance 
against investment in the participatory venture , till the time such contract is initiated.  
Subsequent measurement  
24. The investment in a participatory venture shall be measured at the end of a financial 
reporting period at carrying amount  (adjusted for additional capital contribution, capital 
withdrawals and payment of distributable profits) and shall be adjusted to include the net 
share of the profit or loss of the participatory  venture  as attributable to the investor  under 
the contractual arrangements  (as determined under the relevant AAOIFI FAS s18), as at that 
date.   
25. Net share of the profits and losses  (duly comprising  the net result of r evenues, expenses, 
gains and losses ) arising from investment in a participatory venture  shall be determined 
after allocation of the working partner ’s share of profit (including variable profit ). 
26. Any share of losses reimbursable by the working partner  as established in line with Shari’ah 
principles and rules, due to the working partner ’s negligence and misconduct shall be 
recognised as a reduction in losses (or as a profit, where applicable) when determined as 
such . 
27. The investor shall determine, at each period end, impairment in the value of investment in a 
participatory  venture  in line with the requirements of respective AAOIFI FAS19. The 
settlement risk, as well as, existence of onerous commitments (e.g., a guarantee or a sale 
undertaking), shall also be considered for impairment  assessment  purposes.

28. Share of profits (and / or losses) arising from investment in participatory venture shall be

Accounting and financial reporting in the books of account of the  working 
partner  
31. The working partner  shall apply any of the following approaches for accounting and financial 
reporting of its interest in a participatory venture :  
a. in case the participatory venture is controlled  by the investor(s) only – off-balance 
sheet accounting approach  as prescribed in paragraphs 32-37;  
b. in case the participatory venture is controlled by the working partner  only – quasi -
equity accounting approach  as prescribed in paragraphs 38-46; or  
c. in case the participatory venture is under shared control of the investor(s) and the  
working partner  – the net investment in participatory venture accounting approach ,

 Appendix B: Basis for c onclusions   
Unified standard for accounting for participatory ventures  including 
Musharaka and Mudaraba   
BC1 AAOIFI Accounting Board (AAB / the board ) evaluated the need , suitability  and practicality of 
prescribing accounting requirements  for participatory ventures based on  Musharaka and 
Mudaraba in one standard, as opposed to the earlier issuance of two separate standards  i.e. 
FAS 3 “Mudaraba Financing” and FAS 4 “Musharaka Financing” .  
BC2 The first option considered by the board was to keep accounting and financial reporting for 
each mode under a separate standard , particularly in view of the specific nature of each 
mode in terms of the relationship between the parties / partners involved  and certain 
unique Shari’ah principles and rules applicable respectively , among others . The second 
option was to prescribe accounting principles  on a common basis  for both modes  (as well as, 
any other structure in the nature of participatory venture, irrespective of the nomenclature, 
insofar as it is in line with Shari’ah principles and rules) , on a principle -based approach.  
BC3 The board considered the second option more practical , less confusing and avoiding 
duplication, whereby  all relevant accounting approaches can be applied to different 
participatory ventures while catering to the need of flexibility with regard to the unique 
Shari’ah principles and rules and corresponding profit distribution  mechanism . In doing so, 
accounting for the participatory venture, per se, can also be adequately  defined . 
Working partner ’s / counter -party’s accounting and financial reporting for 
the participatory venture  
BC4 The working group and the board, while initiating the process of revision of the earlier 
standards, noted that both standards included accounting and financial reporting for the 
Islamic financial institution (IFIs / the institutions) making  investment on the basis of either 
Mudaraba or Musharaka. Both the standards , nevertheless,  did not include the counterparty 
accounting , or any details with regard to the accounting and financial reporting by the 
participatory venture itself, which should be the basis of subsequent measurement by both 
parties . 
BC5 The board evaluated as to whether, or not, it would be appropriate (and needed) to include 
the counterparty accounting, as well as, the accounting by the participatory ventures in the 
scope of the standard.  
BC6 After due consideration , the board decided that the revised standard shall additionally  
prescribe the accounting  and financial reporting  requirements in the books of working 
partner(s) and  in respect of  the participatory venture itself .  
BC7 The board decided that inclusion of the counterparty accounting is necessary in line with its 
new accounting standards setting strategy, whereby the counterparty accounting is defined 
for almost all the Islamic finance products, in the relevant standards. This ensures availability 
of the counterparty accounting in line with the Shari’ah principles and rules . This also caters 
to the situation whereby the counterparty is itself a n Islamic  financial institution. In addition, 
it may also cater to the situations  of accounting for Sukuk, in the books of the originator, 

## Page 30

30 
 which are subject to the require ments of the respective AAOIFI FAS36 coupled with the 
accounting and financial reporting requirements applicable to the respective underlying 
product.  
BC8 The board further concluded that the inclusion of the financial reporting requirements for 
the participatory venture itself is necessary to ensure transparency, fairness and ensuring 
compliance with Shari’ah principles and rules. In addition, the same will  ensure appropriate 
subsequen t measurement in the books of the respective parties .

Scoping out : legal  entity structure  
BC9 The board noted that certain participatory ventures including Musharaka and Mudaraba 
take form of a legal entity , and the relevant underlying assets (and liabilities) are segregated 
and / or ring -fenced , particularly including the situations enumerated in paragraph 3(e) .  
BC10  The board evaluated as to whether it would be appropriate, or otherwise, to include 
participatory ventures taking form of a legal entity within the scope of this standard .  
BC11  After due deliberations, the board concluded to scope out legal entities from this standard 
whose accounting and financial reporting requirements are addressed by other AAOIFI FASs 
and the generally accepted accounting principles , in order to keep within the mandate 
assigned to, and strategy for standard setting followed by, the board. Having said that, the 
board agreed to create a limited exception in respect of certain Sukuk structures based on  
participatory structures whereby the back -end accounting for the underlying product has 
been referred back to the relevant AAOIFI FAS(s) . Accordingly, in case of a Sukuk based on a 
participatory structure (e.g., Musharaka or Mudaraba) the back -end accounting shall be in 
line with the requirements of this standard  (including, in particular , the accounting for the 
participatory venture itself) . 
Scoping out : co-owned assets  
BC12  The board noted that in certain jurisdictions, diminishing Musharaka based on Shirkat al -
Milk  (co-ownership) , is a commonly used product . In the past , there had been several 
queries received by the AAOIFI secretariat with regard to the application of FAS 4 
“Musharaka Financing” on such transactions / structure, considering that the said standard 
also addresses diminishing Musharak a, albeit under a different structure based on Shirkat -
ul-Aqd (contractual partnership).  
BC13  The board evaluated as to whether there is a need to address the accounting and financial 
reporting for  such structure under this standard , or to create a specific scope exclusion .  
BC14  The board, after due deliberations , concluded that co -owned assets should be excluded 
from the scope of this standard because the same do not fall within the defini tion of 
Musharaka in the specific context of this standard , and also because the accounting and 
financial reporting requirements in respect of the most  common form of the same (i.e.,

Limited application in respect of  participatory ventures based on Shari’ah compliant 
agricultural products  
BC15  The board considered that theoretically  speaking, Shari’ah compliant participatory ventures 
may include such participatory ventures which are based on certain Shari’ah compliant 
agricultural products, such as Muzara’a, Musaqa and Mughar asa. A question  arises as to 
whether these shall be  subject to similar accounting treatment(s) as prescribed in this 
standard or  shall be subject to different / unique accounting requirements, particularly 
considering that at present there is no specific FAS addressing  the su bject.  While the board 
considered that the principal accounting treatment(s) in respect of such participatory 
ventures may remain in line with those prescribed in this standard, yet, there is a need to 
perform further research on the subject in light of the practical examples (which are rare in 
the current Islamic finance practices).  
BC16  The board considered the pros and cons in respect of including the same categorically in the 
scope of the current project, or to create a scope exclusion in respect of the same.  
BC17  Considering the need for further research, the board concluded  that a separate standard 
needs  to be developed in due course (though not a top priority, considering the limited 
practical use of such products ) and, accordingly,  it would not be appropriate to include the 
same categorically in the scope of this standard. On the contrary, the board further  noted  
that if such  products are specifically scoped out from the standard , then during  the interim 
period till the specific standard is issued the institution s may not  be able to take benefit of 
the principles specified in this  standard , considering the hierarchy defined in the conceptual 
framework38.  
BC18  In view of the above, i t was concluded that the same will not be categorically excluded from 
the scope of this standard, and hence, till issuance of a specific standard on the topic, this 
standard shall be applied insofar as is considered practicable , while complying with the 
specific Shari’ah principles and rules  as applicable to such products . 
Investment vs . financing accounting  approach  
BC19  In contemplating the need to revise the existing standards, the respective working group 
and the board analysed the accounting treatment of participatory ventures  (including 
Musharaka and Mudaraba)  under the generally accepted accounting principles  in different 
markets , as well as, the ir respective  treatment under the existing AAOIFI FAS s in the relevant 
markets .  
BC20  It was noted that under the generally accepted accounting principles, normally, such 
transactions  (particularly, those having an expected profit , e.g., through tiered profit 
mechanism)  are considered as financing transaction s i.e., financial instruments  eligible to be 
accounted for at amortised cost , meeting the SPPI (solely payment of principal and interest )

 test. This is contrary to their true nature as per Shari’ah principles and rules and the usual 
contractual arrangements whereby these are considered as  investment  in (and hence, 
having risk  on) the underlying assets / business operations . It was further noted that the 
names of the previous standards  also mentioned the term “financing” with Mudaraba and 
Musharaka (probably, merely with an intention to distinguish  the same as Islamic banking 
and finance transactions), which at times caused confusion.  
BC21  The board evaluated and concurred with the approach taken by AAOIFI under the previous 
standards whereby such participatory ventures are considered different from conventional 
financial instruments  due to the applicable Shari’ah principles and rules and the contractual 
arrangements under the most common transaction structures. The board also concluded 
that these are also different from traditional joint ventures / joint operations, e.g., in terms 
of profit -sharing mechanisms, Shari’ah requirements, liquidat ion and maturity 
arrangements, diminishing and variability equity structures and the investment motive (as 
opposed to the business motive) , and hence, concluded that the term parti cipatory ventures 
shall be used to distinguish from the same .  
Accounting  approaches  for the investor  
BC22  The board noted that the existing standards follow an approach of accounting for 
participatory ventures (based on Mudaraba or Musharaka) in the books of account of  the 
investor on the basis of performance of the underlying business venture . This was 
considered generally the  appropriate approach in line with the Shari’ah principles and rules 
and the business model of most of such transactions .  
BC23  The board also noted that in most of the practical situations, the working partner enjoys full 
or shared control of the participatory venture , and hence it would be more justified for the 
working partner to record the underlying assets and the results of operation of the 
participatory venture in its books of account, while the investor records its net interest in 
the same in its books . Accordingly, the said approach was decided to  be termed as net 
investment in participatory venture approach, further refine d in line with the newer AAOIFI 
FASs and the  conceptual  framework and maintained as the primary accounting approach for 
the investors .  
BC24  However, t he board took a considered view that in the event an investor  (or a group of 
investors)  solely controls a participatory venture, the most appropriate accounting 
treatment would be pass -through accounting approach , whereby the underlying assets and / 
or results of business operations shall be directly incorporated (or consolidated  / 
proportionately consolidated ) in the books of account of the investor (s). The board was of 
the view that such treatment provides a more fair and transparent depiction of the financial 
information  (and the relevant risks and rewards)  and is better  aligned with the requirements 
of th e conceptual framework and those of AAOIFI FASs39 addressing similar transactions / 
situations.

 Impracticability of pass -through accounting approach  
BC25  Nevertheless, it was  also brought to the attention of the board that in certain  circumstances, 
the application of the pass -through accounting approach by the investor may not be 
practicable. Such situations may arise e.g., when the investor is unable to determine 
proportionate assets (and liabilities) whereby multiple investors are i nvolved, or where the 
working partner’s / participatory venture’s financial reports and information is not available 
or inaccessible at all or  is not available on a timely basis . 
BC26  While the board decided that if the pass -through accounting approach is deemed 
impracticable, then net investment in participatory venture accounting approach shall be 
applied, it still faced a question as to whether or not there is merit in  keep ing the pass -
through accounting approach as the primary / benchmark treatment in situations  whereby 
the investor solely controls the participatory venture . 
BC27  The board considered  that while keeping only the net investment in participatory venture 
approach might simplify the accounting requirements, there are certain significant benefits 
of keeping the pass -through accounting approac h, including  managing the risk of misleading 
financial reporting .  
Accounting  approaches  for the working partner  
BC28  As the previous standards did not include the accounting treatment in the books of the 
investor, the board was seized with the  matter of  prescribing the appropriate approach (es) 
for the same in line with the requirements of the  conceptual  framework  and the approach 
followed by the newer AAOIFI FASs (which is generally aligned with the generally accepted 
accounting principles).  
BC29  At the outset any approach for recording the same as owned assets and a corresponding 
financial liability was ruled out by the board , due to the consideration of the requirements of 
the conceptual  framework  and those of the similar AAOIFI FASs , Shari’ah principles and rules 
and the general business mode of such transactions. Having said that, the board still had 
several accounting approaches to choose  within, including off-balance -sheet accounting 
approach, quasi -equity accounting approach and net investment in participatory venture 
accounting approach.  
BC30  The board evaluated that defining a single accounting approach may not be deem ed 
appropriate and decided that the choice of the respective appropriate accounting approach 
for the working partner shall be based on the extent of the, control exercised by the working 
partner on the participatory venture. The board concluded  that this shall be in line with the 
approach followed in case of AAOIFI FASs applicable on similar transactions duly considering 
the requirements related to control as contained in the resp ective AAOIFI FAS and the 
conceptual framework.  
BC31  Accordingly, it was concluded that:  
a. in respect of a participatory venture controlled by the investor(s) only (and hence , 
not controlled  by the working partner ), the off -balance sheet accounting approach 
shall be applied under which a working partner does not recognise the underlying

34 
 assets and / or liabilities or the results of operation of the participatory venture in its 
books of account , on the premise that it would not be justified to record assets, 
liabilities, results of operation, etc. , in respect of a participatory venture that is not 
controlled;   
b. in respect of a participatory venture controlled solely by the working partner, the 
quasi -equity accounting approach shall be applied  under which the underlying assets 
and / or liabilities or the results of operation of the participatory venture  are 
incorporated in the books of account of the working partner (as these are under its 
control) while recording the share attributable to the investor(s) as quasi -equity ; 
c. in respect of the situations of gradual transfer of ownership  and variable -equity 
participatory ventures , the quasi -equity  accounting shall be applicable (generally 
and always, respectively), on the premise that a shared control exists and it is 
expected that the working partner’s share will gradually increase and eventually it 
will be fully controlled by the working partner . Even if it is under a restricted 
Mudaraba arrangement, the shared control on the proportionate  assets exist s and 
the accounting for gradually increasing or variable proportionate consolidation 
would result in unnecessary accounting complexities and signif icant additional cost 
and effort, without corresponding benefit in terms of improved financial reporting  
[Note : Caution needs to be exercised  while assessing any gradual transfer 
mechanism with restricted Mudaraba , so as not to avoid determining sole control of 
the investor  and hence to defy the appropriate accounting treatments and fair and 
transparent reporting ]; and 
d. in respect of a participatory venture jointly controlled by the working partner and 
the investor(s) ( as a situation of shared control) , the net investment in participatory 
venture accounting approach (similar to the one prescribed for the investor) shall be 
considered more appropriate , on the premise that the working partner enjoys a 
principal position of an investor in this situation , irrespective of managing the affairs 
of the same ; 
e. in respect of a perpetual participatory venture, it would be appropriate to account 
for the same as a separate class of equity (while the accounting for underlying assets 
and results of operation of the participatory venture would be generally similar to 
the quasi -equity accounting)  on the premise that the absence of maturity or a put 
option in the hand of the investor makes such investment in the definition of equity.  
Timing of recognition of compensation in case of negligence by the working 
partner  
BC32  The board noted that the determination of profits and losses arising from participatory 
ventures (for both, the investor and the working partner), and hence the subsequent 
measurement of the participatory venture in the respective books of account, is at t imes 
affected in a peculiar situation whereby a compensation (or reimbursement) against loss 
becomes due from the working partner to the investor. This is the case when a negligence of

BC33  The board considered as to whether this may impact the routine profit and loss computation 
(on a prediction or prudent basis, or any other appropriate basis that may require an accrual 
of the same), or the same shall be considered as an event taking place at a specific time. This 
was necessary in order to prescribe appropriate timing of its recognition in the books of 
account of the investor and the working partner, respectively.  
BC34  After due consideration, the board concluded that the same shall be recorded as an event at

that according to the true nature of a participatory venture, and in line with  the relevant 
Shari’ah principles and rules, such a compensation is determined according to a mechanism 
in line with contractual arrangements (or at time also considering the regulatory 
requirements), and hence, till the time such requirements are not fulf illed, and the same is 
not accordingly determined, a contractual right or obligation does not exist.  
BC35  Having said that, it is important to note that a possible future loss compensation (depending 
on its probability) may be objective evidence of a possible impairment or an onerous 
commitment for the working partner, which shall be considered in line with th e 
requirements of the respective FAS40.

2021, discussed the preliminary study on the revision project.  
H4 The board  held its 23rd meeting on 3 Sha’ban 1442H, corresponding to 16 March 2021, 
where the preliminary study was presented. At this meeting, the board addressed and 
agreed  to the content and structure of the new standard. The board decided to develop one 
standard that covers the various types of participatory ventures falling within its scope.
