Hyperinflationary Economies ” is set out in paragraphs 01 -46. All the paragraphs have equal authority. This 
standard should be read in the context of its objective  and the “AAOIFI Conceptual Framework for Financial 
Reporting ”. 
 
All AAOIFI FAS s shall be read in conjunction with the definitions, Shari’ah principles and rules and key 
considerations provided by AAOIFI Shari’ah Standards  (SSs).

 Preface  
PR1 One of the biggest economic challenges of the current times, particularly in the post COVID -19 
environment , is hyperinflation impacting a number of countries. This economic challenge results  in 
several  accounting and financial reporting related challenges , to the corporate entities, Islamic 
financial institutions  (IFIs / the institutions) being no exception to that.  
PR2 While financial reporting under hyperinflationary circumstances is a huge challenge for all entities, 
certain aspect s originating  from the unique business model, transaction structures and related 
Shari’ah principles and rules, applicable to the IFIs pose bigger challenges  to them . 
PR3 AAOIFI Accounting Board ( AAB  / the board ), considering such unique challenges, decided to develop 
a standard on this area on a priority basis in order to provide guidance to  the institutions operating 
under hyperinflationary economic conditions.

 Introduction  
Overview  
IN1 This standard outlines the principles governing financial reporting , including accounting treatments, 
presentation of financial statements and necessary disclosure s for institutions applying AAOIFI 
Financial Accounting Standards (FASs) , operating within hyperinflationary economies. Th is standard  
is developed taking into account the applicable Shari’ah principles and rules, as well as , the unique 
business models of such  institutions  while stipulating appropriate principles of financial reporting . 
IN2 This standard also prescribes a definition of a hyperinflationary economy and provides guidance on 
as to how to determine whether an economy qualifies as hyperinflationary.  
Rationale for issuing this standard  
IN3 AAOIFI Accounting Board (AAB  / the board ), duly acknowledg es that the economic environment in 
several countries operating in hyperinflationary circumstances, at times, renders the financial 
statements  prepared under AAOIFI FASs, as less meaningful , and not comparable over several 
periods of financial reporting. Accordingly, the board  recognised the need that the institutions 
following AAOIFI FASs in such economic environments, have  to apply specific financial reporting 
requirements making their financial st atements more meaningful and comparable.  
IN4 The board  further realised that certain  financial reporting requirements under the generally 
accepted accounting principles may not suit the institutions  following AAOIFI FASs, considering the 
requirements of relevant Shari’ah principles and rules and their respective business models. 
Accordingly, the board decided to develop a specific standard on this topic.

 AAOIFI Financial Accounting Standard  (FAS) 49  
Financial Reporting for Institutions Operating in Hyperinflationary 
Economies  
Objective of the standard  
 The objective of this standard is to establish the principles of financial reporting for the institutions 
applying AAOIFI Financial Accounting Standards (FASs) operating in hyperinflationary economies, 
duly considering the relevant Shari’ah principles and rules and their unique business models.  
Scope  
 This standard is applicable to the institutions preparing financial statements in line with the 
requirements of AAOIFI FASs, whose functional currency is the currency of a hyperinflationary 
economy.  
 This standard shall also be applicable on consolidated financial statements of an institution to the 
extent of impacts relating to a subsidiary(ies) whose functional currency (ies)  is the currency (ies)  of a 
hyperinflationary economy(ies).  
Determination of a hyperinflationary economy  
Definition of a hyperinflationary economy  
 A hyperinflationary economy is an economy wherein money (normally denoted in terms of the 
currency of that economy) loses purchasing power (against assets, goods and services) at such a rate 
that impedes the meaningful comparison of financial reporting in respect of transactions and events 
that have occurred at different times, sometimes within the same financial reporting period.  
Pertinent factors for determination of a hyperinflationary economy  
 Determination of a hyperinflationary economy, in line with the definition provided in paragraph 4, is 
highly subjective, and hence cannot be performed by applying a fixed or predetermined criteria. 
Nevertheless, any or a combination of the following factors may lead to a conclusion that an 
economy is hyperinflationary:  
a. cumulative inflation rate over a three years’ period has exceeded 100% or is approaching the 
same with an increasing trend;  
b. benchmark / discount rates, wages and prices are linked to an index;  
c. prevalent use of a stable foreign currency in place of functional currency;  
d. prices are usually quoted in a stable foreign currency;  
e. general population prefers to convert / keep monetary assets to / in a stable foreign 
currency; and / or  
f. general population prefers to invest in non -monetary assets and keep minimal amounts of 
monetary assets.

 Notwithstanding the factors enumerated in paragraph 5, an economy may be declared to be 
hyperinflationary by a multilateral institution1 or the respective central bank or the respective 
government.  
Definitions  
 For the purpose of interpreting and applying this standard, the following short definitions are 
relevant:  
a. Fair value – is the price that would be received to sell an asset or paid to transfer a liability in 
an orderly transaction between market participants at the measurement date, and in the 
specific context of this standard and for the purpose of application  of paragraphs 11-29, also 
includes  deemed fair value as prescribed in this standard  (see paragraphs 15 -16); 
b. Hyperinflationary economy – is as defined in paragraph 4;  
c. Index – is the means of quantifying the periodic effect of weighted aggregate price, 
purchasing power and / or performance of economic resources. In the specific context of 
this standard, it is the means of quantifying the periodic effects of weighted aggregate loss 
of purchasing power of a currency against economic resources or against a (or basket of) 
stable foreign currency(ies);  
d. Investment pool – is a virtual entity2, at times a separate reporting entity, comprising distinct 
assets whose risks and rewards (wholly or proportionately) are attributable to the funding 
provided by respective quasi -equity investor (s) [Explanation: investment pool includes the 
general pool, as well as, other specific pools created by an institution .]; 
e. Measuring units – are the units of currency, having a currently prevalent value at the end of 
the financial reporting period, or as indexed at a given point of time in line with the 
requirements of this standard;  
f. Monetary assets – in the specific context of this standard, are assets that are in the form of 
either:  
i. cash;  
ii. contractual right to receive cash or another monetary asset from another institution 
or entity; or  
iii. contractual right to exchange a monetary asset with another institution or entity;  
g. Monetary liabilities – in the specific context of this standard, are the liabilities expected to 
be settled in determinable fixed amounts of money.  
 
1 For example, the International Monetary Fund (IMF)  
2 A virtual entity comprises a distinct set of assets and liabilities pertaining to unique set of stakeholders, without taking 
the form of a legal entity.

 Financial reporting for the institutions operating in a hyperinflationary 
economy(ies)  
Options for financial reporting for the institutions operating in a hyperinflationary 
economy(ies)  
 The application of financial reporting on historical cost basis is not considered appropriate in a 
hyperinflationary economy, as the same does not faithfully represent the financial information 
current at the date of financial statements and the period cov ered thereunder. Accordingly, an 
institution operating in a hyperinflationary economy shall either:  
a. redetermine its functional currency in line with the provisions of paragraph 9; or  
b. follow the fair value based financial reporting requirements according  to paragraph 10.  
Redetermination of functional currency  (and reporting currency)  
 Where a factor, or a combination thereof, as enumerated in paragraphs 5(c), 5(d) and / or 5(e) 
exist(s), and there are no legal or regulatory restrictions to the contrary, an institution shall consider 
as to whether a redetermination of its functional curr ency  (and the reporting currency)  to a stable 
foreign currency is appropriate in line with relevant AAOIFI FASs3. In such a case, the institution shall 
follow the requirements of relevant AAOIFI FASs4.  
Fair value based financial reporting  
 In case of application of paragraph 8(b), t he elements of financial statements of an institution, 
whose functional currency is the currency of a hyperinflationary economy, shall be restated 
according to the requirements contained in paragraphs 11 -29.  
Fair value based financial reporting under hyperinflationary circumstances  
Statement of financial position  
 An institution, whose functional currency is the currency of a hyperinflationary economy, shall 
remeasure items constituting the elements of its statement of financial position at fair value, with 
the exception of accounting treatments prescribed in paragr aphs 12 -13. 
Exceptions to fair value accounting model  
 The following items shall not be measured at fair value :  
a. monetary assets  and monetary liabilities ; 
b. assets acquired for future delivery for which a contract or promise exists requiring delivery / 
sale at a pre -agreed / pre -promised value5;

 c. held -to-maturity investments in Sukuk and similar instruments , reflecting a fixed or virtually 
fixed stream of cash  flows ; 
d. assets and liabilities linked by agreement (subject to Shari’ah principles and rules) to 
changes in prices, such as through an index (with or without caps and floors); and  
e. quasi -equity.  
 The items that are not required to be adjusted to fair value , in line with the requirements of 
paragraph 12, shall be accounted for as follows:  
a. monetary assets , monetary liabilities, held -to-maturity investments and assets acquired for 
future delivery for which a contract or  a promise exists requiring delivery / sale at a pre -
agreed / pre -promised value – to be reported at carrying value in line with the requirements 
of relevant AAOIFI FASs;  
b. assets and liabilities linked by agreement (subject to Shari’ah principles and rules) to 
changes in prices, such as through an index (with or without caps and floors) – to be 
reflected at values adjusted in accordance with the agreement; and  
c. quasi -equity – to be reflecting the appropriate effects of fair value adjustments to the 
underlying assets (and related liabilities, where applicable), either through allocated profits 
or losses or through creation of appropriate reserves.  
Application of fair value and other valuation approaches  
 The items of the statement of financial position of an institution that do not meet the exception 
criteria specified in paragraph 12, except for the transition effects as described in paragraph s 44-46, 
shall include the following:  
a. assets (and liabilities, if any) that are carried at fair value in line with their respective 
accounting policies – to continue being reported at fair values in line with respective 
accounting policies, whereby, the respective gains and losses to be report ed accordingly;  
b. assets (and liabilities, if any) that are carried at cost (or cost less depreciation / amortisation) 
in line with their respective accounting policies – to be reported at fair values determined in 
accordance with this standard, whereby, the respective gains and losses on fair valuation are 
to be reported as a part of net hyperinflationary fair value gain (or loss) being an item of the 
other comprehensive income;  
c. assets that are carried at lower of cost and net realisable value – to be reported at the net 
realisable value being an estimate of fair value only in the specific context of this standard, 
whereby, the respective gain on fair valuation to be reported as a  part of net 
hyperinflationary fair value gain (or loss) being an item of the other comprehensive income, 
and the loss to be reported in line with respective accounting policies;

 d. assets that are carried at the revaluation model (revalued amount less accumulated 
depreciation and accumulated impairment) – to be reported at periodically revalued value, 
at least at the end of the financial year. The resulting gain or loss shall be reco gnised based 
on the respective accounting policy ; and  
e. all other assets (and liabilities) – to be reported at deemed  fair values estimated  through 
application of an index in line with the requirements of paragraph s 15-16, whereby the 
respective gain (or loss ) on fair valuation to be reported as a part of net hyperinflationary 
fair value gain (or loss) being an item of the other comprehensive income . 
Deemed fair value(s)  
 It may be impracticable for an institution to ascertain fair value(s) of certain assets or liabilities due 
to: 
a. unavailability of observable  data /  inputs to assess the fair value(s) of these assets or 
liabilities; or  
b. undue cost and / or effort inevitably to be incurred in ascertaining fair value(s).  
 When the condition(s) described in paragraph 15 apply, only in the specific context of this standard, 
an institution may apply  (the effect of change in ) an index to the balances of such assets and 
liabilities to obtain a deemed fair value.  
Impairment and credit losses  
 The provisions of FAS 30 “Impairment, Credit Losses and Onerous Commitments ” shall apply to the 
restated amounts, especially in case of application of paragraph 16.  
Deemed fair value(s)  for interim financial reporting period(s)  
 For certain assets (and liabilities, where applicable), whereby, the fair value estimation in line with 
requirements of paragraphs 11-14 is performed at the end of the financial year, the requirements of 
paragraphs 15-16 for determination of deemed fair value may still be applicable for the purpose of 
valuation of such assets (or liabilities) as of the end of an interim financial reporting period , if the 
impact within such perio d is considered material . 
Selection and use of an index  
 In line with the requirements of paragraph 16, an institution may be required to use an index that 
reflects changes quantifying the periodic effect of weighted aggregate price, purchasing power and / 
or performance of economic resources. It is preferable that this index is the same index as used by 
other institutions that report in the currency of the same economy.  
 For the purpose of this standard, an index may comprise of a general price index (or a basket of 
general price indices) or an index based on a (or a basket of) stable foreign currency(ies) that 
quantifies the periodic effects of weighted aggregate loss of purchasing power of the functional 
currency.  
 An institution shall assess the appropriateness of the index based on the types and classes of assets 
and liabilities the institution carr ies on its financial statements.

 Statement of income and other comprehensive income  
 The statement of income and other comprehensive income (or statements of income and statement 
of other comprehensive income) for the current financial reporting period shall not be restated at 
the end of the financial reporting period. It shall reflect the  actual revenues and expenses that were 
incurred and recognised.  
Gain or loss arising on the remeasurement  
 The net gain or loss for the period, arising due to application of paragraph s 11 and 13  shall be 
recognised in the statement of other comprehensive income. The resulting item shall either be:  
a. subsequently accumulated as a distinct “hyperinflationary fair value reserve”, categori sed 
separately representing the owners’ equity and the quasi -equity , respectively  (see 
paragraphs  31(e) and 3 4); or 
b. allocated  as profit or loss  for the period  (see paragraphs  31(d) and 32 ).  
Statement of income and attribution related to quasi -equity  
 This standard does not require any adjustment to the statement of income and attribution related to 
quasi -equity, except for reflecting the appropriate effects of fair value adjustments (or adjustments 
referred  to in paragraph 1 3) to the underlying assets (and related liabilities, where applicable). The 
effect of such adjustments shall either be accounted for through allocation of profits, or creation of 
appropriate reserves (including, where applicable, hyperinflationary fair val ue reserve), in 
accordance with the respective accounting policies.  
Statement of cash flows  
 This standard does not require any adjustments to the statement of cash flows.  
Consolidated financial statements  
Restatement in respect of subsidiary(ies) operating in hyperinflationary economy(ies)  
 A parent institution  whose functional currency  is the currency of a hyperinflationary economy may 
have subsidiary(ies)  that also has functional currency(ies)  being  the curren cy(ies) of a 
hyperinflationary econom y(ies). Alternatively, a parent  institution  whose functional currency  is not 
the currency of a hyperinflationary economy may have subsidiary(ies)  that has functional 
currency(ies)  being  the curren cy(ies) of a hyperinflationary econom y(ies). The financial statements 
of any such subsidiary need to be restated by applying the provisions of this standard to the 
balances before they are included in the consolidated financial statements issued by its parent  
institution . Where such a subsidiary is preparing its financial statements in a currency that is 
different from the functional  currency of the parent institution,  its restated financial statements 
shall be translated applying only the closing foreign exchange rates.  
 The financial statements of a subsidiar y(ies) that has functional currency(ies)  not being  the 
curren cy(ies) of a hyperinflationary econom y(ies) are dealt with in accordance with the 
requirements of respective AAOIFI FASs6. 
 
6 FAS 1 “General Presentation and Disclosures in the Financial Statements” and FAS  23 “Consolidation ”

 When financial statements with different period ends are consolidated, this standard shall apply to 
all balances at the date of the consolidated financial statements.  
Restatement of investee institutions  
 An investee that is accounted for under the equity method may have functional currency(ies)  being  
the curren cy(ies) of a hyperinflationary economy. The statement of financial position and statement 
of comprehensive income of such an investee are restated in accordance with this standard in order 
to calculate the investor’s share of its net assets and profit or loss. When t he restated financial 
statements of the investee are expressed in a foreign currency , the same  are translated at the 
closing rates.  
Economies ceasing to be hyperinflationary  
 When an economy ceases to be hyperinflationary and an institution  discontinues the preparation 
and presentation of financial statements prepared in accordance with this standard, it shall treat the 
amounts  reported in line with the requirements of this standard, at the end of the previous financial 
reporting period , as the basis for the carrying amounts in its subsequent financial statements.  
Presentation and disclosures  
 In addition to the presentation and disclosure requirements stated in FAS 1 “General Presentation 
and Disclosures in the Financial Statements”, unless contradictory to any specific requirement of this 
standard, an institution shall disclose the following:  
a. the fact that the institution’s functional currency is the currency of a hyperinflationary 
economy and hence, it has followed the requirements of this standard for the purpose of 
preparation of its financial statements;  
b. the fact that the financial statements are based on fair value based financial reporting under 
hyperinflationary circumstances for the current period and the corresponding figures (see 
transitional provisions prescribed in paragraph s 44-46) with additional disclosures of 
indexed comparative figures for the previous period;  
c. the identity and level of the index at the end of the financial reporting period and the 
movement in the index during the current and the previous financial reporting period ; and  
d. the amount of the net hyperinflationary fair value gain or loss for the period, presented in 
the statement of other comprehensive income, arising due to application of paragraph s 11 
and 13 that is:  
i. allocated as profit or loss (distributable or non-distributable ) to the quasi -equity; 
and 
ii. allocated as profit or loss (distributable or non-distributable ) to the owner’s equity ; 
and

Accounting policies  
 An institution shall disclose new and amended accounting policies as a result of application of this 
standard and their respective impacts including, in particular, the accounting policy(ies) related to 
accounting for (and recycling, where applicable) of t he hyperinflationary fair value reserve, and its 
attribution to (and to the classes of) owners’ equity and quasi -equity.

 Corresponding and indexed comparative figures  
 An institution shall present the corresponding  figures for its financial statements  the same  as 
previously reported (except for any restatements in line with the relevant requirements of FAS 1 
“General Presentation and Disclosures in the Financial Statements ”).  
 An institution shall additionally present a third  column representing the indexed comparative figures 
by applying an index  to the corresponding figures  in line with the requirements of paragraph s 19-21. 
Computation of indexed comparative figures  
 Indexed comparative figures shall be computed in the following manner:  
a. in respect of balances reported in the statement of financial position, by applying the 
changes in index from the date of the end of the previous financial reporting period and the 
end of the current financial reporting period; and  
b. in respect of items of the financial statements other than the balances reported in the 
statement of financial position, by applying the changes in index from the date (or week or 
month, as practicable) of the respective transaction(s) to the end of the cu rrent financial 
reporting period.  
Effective date  
 This standard shall be effective for the financial periods beginning on or after 1 January 2026. Early 
adoption is encouraged.  
 It is preferable that all institutions operating in the same hyperinflationary economy shall apply this 
standard from the same date to ensure that comparability between their results is possible.  
Transitional provisions  
 Notwithstanding the requirements of FAS 1 “General Presentation and Disclosures in the Financial 
Statements ”, the application of this standard shall be on a modified retrospective basis (without 
restatement of corresponding figures and taking the cumulative effects in the opening retained 
earnings / reserves) effective from the later of:  
a. beginning of the annual financial reporting period in which the institution concludes that the 
economy is hyperinflationary in nature; or  
b. beginning of the annual financial reporting period in which this standard is adopted for the 
first time.  
 The application of this standard on the modified retrospective basis as provided in paragraph 4 4 
without restatement of the corresponding figures shall not preclude the institution to apply the 
requirement of paragraph 3 8 and disclose the additional column of indexed comparative figures for 
the previous period.  
 The application of modified retrospective basis according to paragraph 4 4 shall disclose the 
appropriate adjustment for attribution to quasi -equity. The disclosure shall be according to 
requirements of paragraph 3 1(e).

 Appendix B: Basis for conclusion  
Adoption of a financial reporting approach different from generally accepted accounting 
principles  
BC1 AAOIFI Accounting Board (AAB / the board ), at the outset , explore d and analysed internally, as well 
as, through extensive research and interaction with various stakeholders, option s to adopt or adapt 
the accounting treatments stipulated in the generally accepted accounting principles , to be applied 
on the institutions complying with the AAOIFI Financial Accounting Standards (FASs) . 
BC2 This course of action, however, entailed additional complexities for both accounting and Shari’ah 
domains , particularly considering the unique business model of the institutions, as well as, their 
respective transaction structure. Notably, these complexities included the utili sation of an 
appropriate index, the methodology for distributing reserves and the treatment and disclosure of 
comparative balances, particularly in situations where quasi -equity was constructively liquidated on 
a periodic basis .  
BC3 This viewpoint was subsequently validated by the Shari’ah committee7, which conducted an in -
depth evaluation of the treatments specified in the generally accepted accounting principles, and 
the observations of the board thereon , ultimately deeming many of them incompatible with 
Shari’ah principles .  
BC4 As a result, the board opted for an out of the box approach in the development of this standard , 
instead of applying the general strategy of the board to harmonise the AAOIFI FASs with the 
generally accepted accounted principles, as far as practicable.  Such out of the box solution included 
a third column in certain primary statements and related notes, to provide better comparisons 
without adjusting the opening balances.  
Pertinent factors for determination of a hyperinflationary economy  
BC5 The board deliberated , at length , the factors to determine  a hyperinflationary economy. It was 
observed that one of the commonly referenced factors within the accounting fraternity is the 
cumulative inflation rate surpassing 100% over a span of three years .  
BC6 There was a consensus that additional clarification was imperative, as establishing a fixed threshold 
(i.e., 100%) as the benchmark cumulative inflation rate over three years could , at times,  potentially 
lead to ambiguity. As generally speaking, a  cumulative rate of precisely 100% would necessitate the 
adoption of this standard, while a slightly lower rate (e.g., 99%) might permit institutions to 
continue with the regular accounting framework.   
BC7 In order t o mitigate such potential confusion , the board took a view that i n cases where the 
inflation trajectory is ascending  while cumulating to 100% or near to that over a three years ’ period 
(e.g., an inflation rate of 25%, 34% and 40% in year s 1, 2 and  3, respectively ), indicating an upward 
trend, the same shall be regarded as an indicator of hyperinflation. Conversely, if there is a 
descending trend with the inflation rate declining  (even if nearing 100% cumulative inflation over a

 period of 3 years (e.g., 40%, 35% and 24% in years 1, 2, and 3 respectively ), the same may not be 
deemed to demonstrate hyperinflation . 
Authority to determine economy as hyperinflationary  
BC8 The board  noted that in certain jurisdictions, even when the cumulative inflation rate surpassed 
100% over a three  year s’ period, the competent authorities did not officially classify the economy as 
hyperinflationary.  
BC9 The board recogn ised that in these instances, a potential issue could emerge where some 
institutions opt to prepare their financial statements with hyperinflationary adjustments, while 
others adhere to regulatory guidelines. This divergence in approaches could compromise the 
principle of comparability among financial statements . 
BC10  It was concluded that the criteria specified in paragraph 5 shall aid in assessing the relevance of this 
standard. However, paragraph 6 provides the flexibility for such  pronouncement by a government, 
central bank or a multilateral institution to take precedence.  
BC11  However, it was clarified that this standard shall not be applicable if the relevant regulatory 
authority does not adopt this standard . 
Redetermination of functional currency  
BC12  The board deliberated on the significance of factors enumerated in paragraphs 5(c), 5(d) and  / or 
5(e) within an economy. The  existence of these factors suggest s that the functional currency has 
relinquished its significance, and a stable foreign currency has, informally, assumed its role.   
BC13  The board  also acknowledged that certain institutions adopt a practice of preparing financial 
statements in both the functional currency and a stable foreign currency, presenting them side by 
side in two columns .  
BC14  The board deliberated on the possibility of providing the option to change the functional currency 
of financial statements to a stable foreign currency. The board recogn ised that such a decision 
carries both advantages and disadvantages . However, after due deliberations, t he board concluded 
that transparency and comparability is better achieved when financial statements are presented in 
the currency predominantly used for transactions  (directly, or through pricing thereof) . Considering 
the Shari’ah compliance and business models related issues in adoption of generally accepted 
accounting principles, the board concluded that one of the possible options is to change the 
functional currency, insofar as, the same is allowable  under regulatory requirements.  
BC15  The board clarified  that, in the event of a change in the functional currency by an institution in 
accordance with paragraph 9 of this standard, the institution shall not apply the requirements 
outlined in paragraph 10 and remainder of the standard . 
Fair value based financial reporting  
BC16  The board deliberated on the advantages of harmon ising  this standard with the generally accepted 
accounting principles, as far as practicable . Such principles involve util ising  indexation of non -
monetary assets and liabilities to reflect the impacts of inflation across the elements of financial 
statements . These principles also allow use of current cost convention (e.g., by applying fair values ) 

## Page 19

19 
 for certain items of financial statements which , in such case , are not required to be adjusted for 
indexation.  
BC17  It was also noted that employing a price index to report non -monetary assets might not consistently 
ensure a faithful representation in respect of all the relevant elements within financial statements. 
As an example, certain a ssets , like real estate, tend to appreciate in market value over extended 
periods of inflation , although , in shorter -term scenarios, such assets could experience decline in 
value , due to market factors other than inflation . 
BC18  As a result, it was determined that employing the fair value model for financial statement 
preparation offers greater transparency , as well as, comparability  for the users of the financial 
statements. While this approach may diverge partially from the generally accepted accounting 
principles applicable to hyperinflation, the board chose to adopt it within this standard as an  out of 
the box solution , as it is in greater conformity with the objectives of financial reporting .  
BC19  The board further resolved to emphas ise the importance of institutions ensuring that fair value 
encompasses considerations and assumptions that market participants would employ when valuing 
assets or liabilities .  
Deemed f air value  
BC20  The board noted that the term "fair value" enjoys widespread recognition within a specific context. 
AAOIFI FASs, as well as, generally accepted accounting principles provide a clear definition of "fair 
value" and categor ise its measurement into three distinct levels  of inputs . 
BC21  The notion that , in certain circumstances,  the indexed value can serve as an approximation to the 
fair value, particularly when obtaining the actual fair value is challenging, theoretically yields values 
nearly approximating  the fair values of assets and liabilities. This is particularly pertinent in 
situations where prices are experiencing rapid fluctuations. However, it  is important to note that 
indexed values are not typically employed in accounting practices for the measurement of assets 
and liabilities unless the economy is character ised by hyperinflationary conditions . 
BC22  The board recogn ised that employing the term "fair value" for indexed values could potentially lead 
to user confusion. Additionally, it was acknowledged that the use of indexed values as approximate 
measurements, while simpler, might be extended to scenarios beyond those cha racter ised by 
hyperinflation.  
BC23  Consequently, the board opted for a cautious approach when considering the concept of indexed 
values approximating the fair value of an asset (or liability ). To eliminate ambiguity, the board 
decided to introduce the term "deemed fair value" specifically for cases where indexed values are 
employed as approximations to fair value within the context of hyperinflationary economies.  
Exceptions to fair value accounting model  
BC24  The board consider ed the views raised by certain stakeholders in the working group meetings and 
broader stakeholders’ interactions that t he fair value model might not be suitable for all assets and 
liabilities. Certain  assets are already reported on the statement of financial position (SOFP) using 
the fair value model. Additionally, it would not be fitting to employ fair valuation for assets held as 
inventory intended for future delivery.

 BC25  The board also examined the particular scenario of held -to-maturity (HTM) investments in Sukuk 
and similar instruments character ised by fixed or nearly fixed cash flows. It was noted that the value 
of HTM investments can fluctuate due to changes in fair valuation (caused by higher profit rates 
offered on newer instruments), shifts in currency values, or a combination of both factors 
simultaneously . 
BC26  It was noted that transitioning from the amort ised cost to the fair value method for valuation would 
entail an initial loss (or, rarely a gain) for HTM investments  (particularly, those having fixed rate of 
return) . However, these gains and losses would offset each other over the investment's maturity 
period, ultimately resulting in the institution receiving the par value in cash.  
BC27  Additionally, it was noted that HTM investments typically form a substantial portion of an 
institution's investment portfolio. Any decrease in their value could negatively impact the 
institution's ability to continue operations as a going concern, and it m ay also affect compliance 
with regulatory capital maintenance requirements . 
BC28  Furthermore, the board recogn ised that, according to generally accepted accounting principles , 
HTM investments are treated as monetary assets , and hence are not subject to indexation under 
the hyperinflationary financial reporting requirements . Taking into account the advantages and 
disadvantages discussed earlier, the board determined that HTM investments in Sukuk and similar 
instruments should be treated as an exception to the fair value accounting model .  
BC29  A comparable decision was reached concerning the Ijarah Muntahia Bittamleek assets that are 
committed to being delivered to customers in the future. These assets, owned by the institution, 
typically appreciate in value. Nevertheless, since the selling pric e at the end of the Ijarah term, or at 
various points throughout the contract, is typically pre -determined, recogn ising  a gain on the Ijarah 
asset during the Ijarah term, followed by disposing of the asset at a loss, would not accurately 
depict the transac tion's essence.   
Treatment of quasi -equity  
BC30  Quasi -equity presents a distinctive challenge in the context of hyperinflation. It represents a 
financial statement component that embodies characteristics primarily that of equity, but also that 
of a liability.  
BC31  The board deliberated on multiple options for dealing with quasi -equity in a hyperinflationary 
economy. One approach under consideration was to classify it as a liability, thereby categor ising  it 
as a monetary asset – consequently falling within the exceptions to the fair value accounting model 
as outlined in paragraph 12(a). However, this approach, while straightforward to implement, was 
dismissed due to its inconsistency with  its classification under the AAOIFI framework and other 
relevant AAOIFI FASs . Considering all such issues, the board opted for an approach in which quasi -
equity is not directly valued (or indexed) but is adjusted to reflect only the adjustments made to the 
underlying assets  applying the relevant profit and loss distribution approach .  
BC32  The board also considered that certain profits (mainly comprising of the fair value gains) may not be 
distributable immediately due to various considerations ( particularly , liquidity constraints), and 
hence shall be kept in the hyperinflationary fair value reserves (duly attributable to the respective 
stakeholders). The board further concluded that th e distribution of these reserves pertains to

 Shari’ah and governance  considerations , as well as, contractual  arrangements  rather than 
accounting procedures. In order to maintain transparency, the board determined that a disclosure 
requirement should be in place to outline the strategy for util ising  or distributing the 
hyperinflationary fair value reserve , as well as, information about their expected reversal, where 
applicable . This ensures that quasi -equity holders are informed about the balances that could 
potentially be returned to them in the futur e. 
Statement of cash flows  
BC33  The board extensively deliberated  on the utility of including indexed comparative balances for the 
statement of cash flows.  
BC34  It was noted that the statement of cash flows primarily serves as a presentation statement. The 
board remained unconvinced that introducing a third column with indexed comparative figures 
would enhance the value of information for the users .  
BC35  The board intentionally retained flexibility in the standard to facilitate financial statement 
preparers. It was decided that if management  of the institution  or regulators find the presentation 
of indexed comparative figures beneficial, it may include an additional column to be presented in 
the statement of cash flows .  
Consolidated financial statements  
BC36  The working group deliberated on whether this standard should be applicable to an institution that 
operates in a non -hyperinflationary economy but has a subsidiary reporting in a hyperinflationary 
currency.  
BC37  The board observed that the conceptual framework applies to institutions, encompassing all their 
components. Consequently, the standard is relevant to a subsidiary reporting in a hyperinflationary 
currency, even if the parent institution does not report in a hyperinflationary currency.  
BC38  The board  decided to  provide clarification for situations where a subsidiary operates in a country 
not declared as hyperinflationary. In these cases, for consolidation  purposes , the parent institution 
must assess the subsidiary using the criteria outlined in paragraph 5 .  
Economies ceasing to be hyperinflationary  
BC39  The board deliberated that practical difficulties may arise in the adoption of accounting policies 
following the determination that an economy has ceased to be hyperinflationary , and hence, 
considered the need of providing additional guidance to compliment the requirements of 
paragraph 30 . 
BC40  The board , after due deliberation, concluded  that the institution should refer to the guidance 
provided in FAS 36 “First -Time Adoption of AAOIFI Financial Accounting Standards”  while  
developing the accounting policies  once  an economy has ceased to be hyperinflationary.

 Appendix C: Brief history of the preparation of the standard  
H1 During the 28th AAOIFI Accounting Board (AAB  / the board ) meeting held on 16 -17 Dhul -Qa’dah 
1443H, corresponding to , 15-16 June 2022, the board agreed on a  need for a standard to address 
accounting in hyperinflationary economies and a working group was formed for this purpose.  
H2 The 1st working group meeting was held on 5 Muharram 1444H, corresponding to , 3 August 2022. In 
this meeting, working group members engaged in discussions on the scope, pertinent issues and 
corresponding Shari’ah implications related to hyperinflationary economies.  
H3 The 2nd working group meeting was held on 12 Muharram 1444H, corresponding to , 10 August 2022 
where the members discussed the major points derived from the generally accepted accounting 
principles applicable on the f inancial reporting in hyperinflationary economies , and Shari’ah 
implications of the same . It was  recommended to  develop  a separate standard on financial reporting  
for hyperinflationary economies .  
H4 The 31st meeting of the  Shari’ah committee8 was held on 23 Muharram 1444H,  corresponding to , 21 
August 2022. In this meeting , Shari’ah issues  arising  on accounting in hyperinflationary economies  
under generally accepted accounting principles were discussed.  
H5 AAB in its 29th meeting held on 29 -30 Safar 1444H, corresponding to , 25-26 September 2022, duly  
considering the views of the Shari’ah  committee,  concluded that the application of the generally 
accepted accounting principles for hyperinflation is not deemed aligned with the AAOIFI Financial 
Accounting Standards (FASs). The board’s decision was  accordingly  published through a press 
release to clarify this matter.  
H6 A round table discussion was held on 8 Rajab 1444H, corresponding to , 30 January 2023 to discuss 
key issues in accounting for hyperinflationary economies by a select group of industry leaders, 
experts and Shari’ah scholars.  
H7 The 3rd - 6th working group meeting s were held between  16 Sha’ban  - 7 Shawwal  1444H, 
corresponding to , 8 March  - 27 April  2023 respectively where extensive discussion s on AAOIFI’s 
proposed model  for financial reporting under  hyperinflation  were held .  
H8 The draft standard was presented to AAB in its 33rd meeting held on 2-3 Dhul -Qa’dah 1444H , 
corresponding  to, 22-23 May 2023 . In this meeting , the board reviewed the draft and  
recommendations made by the working group. After due deliberations, the exposure draft was  
approved for issuance.  
H9 After incorporation of board comments and an additional round of review via circulation by the  
working group, the exposure draft was issued on 26 Safar  1445H, corresponding to , 11 September  
2023. 
H10 Three  public hearings (t wo in English  language  and one in Arabic  language ) for the exposure draft 
were held during  the period between  Ramadan 1445H – Muharram 1446H,  corresponding to , March  
2024  - July 2024.
